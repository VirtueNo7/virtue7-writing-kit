# 2. The Canonical Kernel

The canonical kernel is the smallest approved expression of the project. It must be short enough to inspect and strong enough to govern expansion.

A topic is not a kernel. “A book about craftsmanship” names a subject but does not define what the reader should understand, what changes, why it matters, or what the book refuses to claim.

## Required fields

- **Identity:** working title, project ID, version, status.
- **Source state or central question:** where the reader, problem, subject, or inquiry begins.
- **Target state or intended understanding:** what becomes possible by the end.
- **Mechanism or governing relation:** the causal, logical, chronological, comparative, or instructional structure connecting the book.
- **Reader value:** what the reader can recognise, understand, decide, feel, or do differently.
- **Boundaries:** what the book does not diagnose, prove, promise, imitate, or authorise.
- **Invariant set:** the claims and qualifications that must survive every legitimate adaptation.

## Kernel test

A kernel is ready for review when it can answer:

1. What is this book truly about?
2. What changes for the reader?
3. How or why does that change occur?
4. What must remain true in every version?
5. What interpretation would materially distort the project?

Large-scale drafting may reveal that the kernel needs revision. That change must receive a new version, rationale, and propagation record. Otherwise the project acquires several competing truths and relies on whichever draft happens to look most confident.
